### Analysis of 'Nicolas Bardewieck' by Otto Anthes

#### 1. PLOT SUMMARY

Nicolas Bardewieck, a Lübeck councilman, is dispatched to Odense, Denmark, to secure the confirmation of Lübeck's ancient privileges from the newly crowned Danish king. After successfully obtaining the sealed document after three days of negotiations, he attends a celebratory banquet hosted by the Danish royal councilors. During the meal, his vigilant mounted servant uncovers a plot by the Danes to intoxicate Bardewieck and seize the document back. With the servant's clever assistance in discreetly swapping drinks, Bardewieck feigns drunkenness while the Danish councilors become genuinely inebriated and forget their scheme. Back in his quarters, Bardewieck, physically and emotionally spent, collapses, realizing he is dying. With his last breaths, he entrusts the crucial document to his servant, ordering him to ride immediately and secretly to Lübeck and deliver it to the council, explaining that he, Bardewieck, will follow later, "because the dead do not travel so fast." He dies overnight, having sacrificed his life for his city's welfare.

#### 2. SPATIAL REPRESENTATION OF LÜBECK

The story 'Nicolas Bardewieck' is primarily set in Odense, Denmark, making Lübeck's representation indirect but profoundly significant:

*   **Topographical:** Lübeck itself is not physically depicted, as the narrative unfolds in a foreign land. However, the "Rathaus" (City Hall) is explicitly mentioned as the ultimate destination for the vital document. This signifies Lübeck's administrative and civic heart, a stable and central point of power and authority to which the councilman's mission is directed. The journey *to* Lübeck, undertaken by the servant, represents the return of the city's self-determination and legal standing.
*   **Atmospherically:** Lübeck is portrayed through the lens of its historical importance and its need for defense. The "alten Privilegien" (old privileges) evoke a venerable past and a tradition of independent governance. The atmosphere surrounding Lübeck is one of civic pride, historical legacy, and a persistent, almost precarious, autonomy in the face of external powers (Denmark). The city is implicitly framed as a cherished entity whose rights must be safeguarded at all costs, instilling a sense of responsibility and reverence in its representatives.
*   **Emotionally:** Lübeck is the object of intense loyalty, duty, and ultimate sacrifice. Bardewieck's profound dedication ("Leben oder sterben, das ist alles eins, für Lübeck!") elevates the city to an almost sacred entity for which life itself is a secondary consideration. It embodies home, identity, and a collective heritage worth dying for. The emotional representation is one of an idealized "Heimat," a precious and beautiful city (as suggested by the collection title 'Lübeck du seltsam schöne Stadt') that commands absolute devotion and inspires heroic selflessness.

#### 3. THEMES & MOTIFS

The story 'Nicolas Bardewieck' explores several central themes and motifs:

*   **Civic Duty and Sacrifice:** This is the paramount theme. Bardewieck embodies the ideal of a selfless public servant. His willingness to endanger his health and ultimately give his life for the "Privilegien" of Lübeck underscores the paramount importance of civic duty and patriotic sacrifice. His dying words, "Meiner Stadt hab ich genug getan," affirm this ultimate commitment.
*   **City Identity vs. External Threat (Art vs. Commerce/Power):** The narrative highlights the ongoing struggle of a free imperial city (Lübeck) to maintain its historical rights and autonomy against the encroaching power of a larger monarchy (Denmark). This tension reflects the historical realities of Hanseatic cities navigating complex political landscapes. The "Privilegien" symbolize Lübeck's unique identity, which must be fiercely protected from foreign opportunism.
*   **Tradition and Historical Rights:** The "alten Privilegien" are not mere legal documents but represent the very foundation of Lübeck's historical status and independence. Their confirmation is a validation of tradition and continuity, underscoring the legal and historical basis of the city's self-governance.
*   **Loyalty and Trust:** The relationship between Nicolas Bardewieck and his "Reitender Diener" is crucial. The servant's unwavering loyalty, sharp perception, and clever execution of the drink-swapping scheme are instrumental to the mission's success. This motif highlights the importance of faithful service and trust within a hierarchical structure dedicated to a common goal.
*   **Deception and Diplomacy:** The story vividly portrays the strategic use of deception ("feigned drunkenness") as a tool in high-stakes diplomacy. Bardewieck's ability to outwit his adversaries without direct confrontation, enabled by his servant's wit, showcases a form of cunning essential for political survival. This also explores the psychological burden of maintaining such a façade.
*   **The Power of the Written Word:** The "Schriftstück" itself functions as a potent motif. It is more than just paper and ink; it is the physical embodiment of Lübeck's rights, sovereignty, and historical memory. Its security is the ultimate objective, making it a sacred object for which Bardewieck is prepared to die.
*   **Heroic Death for the City:** Bardewieck's demise is presented not as a tragic end but as a noble and honorable act, a direct contribution to his city's well-being. The concluding lines explicitly frame his death as a form of service, elevating patriotic sacrifice to an admirable and selfless virtue.

---

Otto Anthes's "Nicolas Bardewieck" thus functions as a powerful narrative celebrating civic virtue and the profound connection between an individual and their city, resonating with a romanticized vision of Lübeck's historical significance and the sacrifices made to uphold it.
