### Analysis of 'Die Geschiedenen' by Otto Anthes

**1. PLOT SUMMARY:**
The story 'Die Geschiedenen' (The Divorced Ones) recounts the tumultuous relationship of a farmer from Wulfsdorf and Annemarie from Vorrade, both villages in the Lübeck region. Despite being seemingly well-matched, their marriage quickly devolves into constant quarrels, leading Annemarie to abandon the farm after a year. For two decades, they live separately. When the farmer turns fifty, influenced by a covetous young maid, he decides to initiate divorce proceedings in Lübeck. The legal process in the courthouse leaves him deeply disquieted, as he perceives it as an impersonal, arrogant intrusion into deeply personal matters. He also discerns the maid's true, avaricious nature. After the divorce is granted, he encounters Annemarie on his journey home and offers her a ride. During their hesitant conversation, they reflect on their past and express their frustration with the legal system. A subsequent carriage accident throws them together, forcing them to cooperatively repair the damage. This shared experience rekindles their bond. They decide to reconcile and remarry on their own terms that very evening, viewing any subsequent official ceremonies as mere formalities.

**2. SPATIAL REPRESENTATION OF LÜBECK:**
The spatial representation in 'Die Geschiedenen' hinges on a distinct dichotomy between the rural environs and the urban center of Lübeck.

*   **Topographical:** The story primarily grounds itself in the "Lübischen" (Lübeck region), specifically mentioning the rural villages of **Wulfsdorf** (the farmer's home) and **Vorrade** (Annemarie's original home and where she lives post-separation). These locations represent the characters' rooted, agricultural existence. **Lübeck** itself serves as a specific, yet largely functional, destination. Key urban locations include:
    *   The **Gerichtshaus** (courthouse): The epicentral site of the legal proceedings, symbolizing institutional power.
    *   A **Gasthof** (inn/guesthouse): Where the farmer stays, a temporary, practical stopover.
    *   The "Stadt" and "Tor hinaus": The city's boundaries, emphasizing a clear demarcation between urban administration and rural life.
*   **Atmospherically & Emotionally:** Lübeck is portrayed not as the "seltsam schöne Stadt" implied by the collection's title, but rather as an alienating, bureaucratic space. For the farmer, it is a place of "ungeheuerliche Anmaßung des Gesetzes" and "unkeuschen Vermessenheit der Richter." Emotionally, Lübeck evokes feelings of confusion, anger, and a profound sense of injustice and detachment from genuine human experience. The "nasse Land" and "Schmutz" of the rural road, while physically challenging, are paradoxically the setting for authentic human reconnection and emotional clarity, away from the city's impersonal jurisdiction. The journey *out* of Lübeck and back towards Wulfsdorf becomes a metaphorical journey of self-discovery and relational renewal, highlighting the primacy of the personal sphere over institutional dictates.

**3. THEMES & MOTIFS:**
Several central themes and motifs are woven into 'Die Geschiedenen':

*   **Tradition vs. Modernity/Bureaucracy:** This is arguably the most prominent theme. The story starkly contrasts a traditional, lived understanding of marriage, rooted in personal will and emotional history, with the impersonal, formal, and often alienating processes of modern law and bureaucracy. The farmer's outrage at the court's interference ("Was geht's die eigentlich an") underscores the tension between individual autonomy and institutional authority. The couple's eventual decision to remarry "durch eigenen Spruch" directly challenges and overrides the official legal ruling.
*   **Love, Desire, and Reconciliation:** The narrative explores the complexities of long-term relationships, hidden affections, and the re-evaluation of desire. The farmer's initial, superficial attraction to the "hübsche junge Magd" quickly sours into "tiefe, versonnene Angst" upon realizing her avarice. This contrasts with his rediscovered yearning for Annemarie's deeper, authentic qualities – her "straffen, Schlanken, Hellen, Blonden" and her "verborgenen Feuer." The story emphasizes that true connection often lies beneath layers of misunderstanding and can be rekindled through shared experience and honest communication.
*   **Authenticity vs. Superficiality:** The characters themselves embody this theme. The young maid represents a superficial beauty driven by material gain ("triumphierenden, habgierigen Blick"), ultimately leading to a sense of "unheimliche schwarze, dumme Schönheit." Annemarie, despite her initial fiery temper, possesses an "echte bäuerliche Schönheit" and a deeper, more mysterious inner life ("Geheimnis und Unergründliches"). The legal system itself is depicted as superficial, dealing only with "leere Form" rather than the "tiefsten Blut und Herzen" of the individuals involved.
*   **The Power of Personal Will and Experience:** The story culminates in a powerful affirmation of individual agency. The farmer and Annemarie reclaim their marriage not through legal channels but through a conscious, personal decision forged by shared experience and rediscovered mutual respect. Their self-decreed remarriage symbolizes the triumph of personal conviction over external, imposed judgments.
*   **Rural Life and Values:** Rooted in the "Lübischen," the story valorizes practical, grounded rural life. The characters are "Bauer" (farmer) and "bäuerliche Schönheit" (peasant beauty). The cooperative act of repairing the broken carriage following the accident serves as a symbolic catalyst for their reconciliation, highlighting the practical problem-solving and mutual support inherent in their rural existence, contrasting sharply with the abstract pronouncements of the urban court.
