### Analysis of 'Jürgen Wullenwever' by Otto Anthes

**1. PLOT SUMMARY:**
Jürgen Wullenwever lies tortured and chained in a dungeon in Wolfenbüttel, physically broken and mentally tormented by coerced confessions. He is visited by his jailer, the same man who tortured him, whose "good, almost sad face" strikes Wullenwever. The jailer explains his actions as obedience to the "power of the world." Wullenwever, however, defiantly asserts a "power of truth and justice." He defends his past actions in Lübeck – overthrowing a failing council, utilizing church treasures for war, and introducing a "new faith" – as a "holy duty of necessity" to save his besieged city from encroaching foreign powers. He blames his ultimate failure on "Schicksal" (fate), maintaining his innocence. Refusing a final meal, Wullenwever declares he dies for a "beautiful high bright dream" and wishes to spend his last hour lost in it. Left alone, he experiences a vivid vision of the Dance of Death from St. Marien, recognizing himself as the last figure led by Death.

**2. SPATIAL REPRESENTATION OF LÜBECK:**
Lübeck is primarily represented through Wullenwever's nostalgic and defensive recollections of its former glory and its state of crisis during his rule. Topographically, it is envisioned as the "Hansenhauptstadt" (Hanseatic capital), once "mighty above all others in the entire Baltic Sea region" ("ganzen Umkreis der Ostersee"), now under existential threat from Dutch, Danish, and Swedish powers.

Emotionally, Lübeck is portrayed as a beloved city for which Wullenwever felt an intense loyalty and responsibility. He acted "das Beste wollend" (wanting the best), driven by a "heilige Pflicht der Not" (holy duty of necessity) for its "Wohl und Wehe" (well-being and woe), even if his actions were later perceived to have "broke its bloom." The narrative conveys a deep, personal connection to the city, tinged with the tragedy of his ultimately failed efforts.

A crucial specific location mentioned is the **Kapelle des Totentanzes (Dance of Death Chapel) in St. Marien (Marienkirche)**. This detail anchors the story firmly in Lübeck's rich historical and artistic heritage. Wullenwever’s final vision of the Totentanz, a famous motif in Lübeck’s Marienkirche, is not merely a hallucination but a vivid, culturally specific memory that brings the city's symbolic and architectural presence into his cell, linking his personal fate to the city's historical consciousness and its iconic art.

**3. THEMES & MOTIFS:**
*   **Individual Conscience vs. State Authority:** The central conflict pits Wullenwever's belief in the "power of truth and justice" and his "holy duty of necessity" against the "power of the world" represented by the Duke and the jailer following orders. The story questions the legitimacy of authority when it fails its people and the personal cost of defying it.
*   **Historical Interpretation and Justification:** Wullenwever's lengthy self-defense attempts to rewrite his historical legacy, portraying his controversial actions (overthrowing the council, using church treasures, religious reform) not as crimes but as heroic, desperate measures to save Lübeck. This theme explores how history is judged and the often-subjective nature of guilt and innocence in times of crisis.
*   **Martyrdom and Idealism:** Wullenwever refuses a last meal, declaring he dies "für einen schönen hohen hellen Traum" (for a beautiful high bright dream). This elevates his impending execution to a form of martyrdom for his ideals and unwavering vision for Lübeck, rather than a punishment for misdeeds.
*   **Fate vs. Guilt:** Wullenwever attributes the loss of his war and the subsequent decline of Lübeck's "Blüte" (bloom) to "Schicksal" (fate) rather than his own "Schuld" (guilt), raising profound questions about free will, responsibility, and the uncontrollable forces that shape human endeavors.
*   **Religion and Internalized Faith:** Wullenwever's statement, "Es gibt keinen alten und keinen neuen Glauben... Es gibt nur einen Glauben. Das ist der, der sich einem im tiefsten Herzen bezeugt," reflects the deeply personal and internal spiritual experience characteristic of the Reformation era, contrasting with institutional dogma.
*   **The "Good" Torturer:** The motif of the jailer with a "good, almost sad face" who is merely following orders complicates the traditional antagonist role. He represents the individual caught in the machinery of "Gewalt der Welt," highlighting the human element within systemic cruelty and the banality of evil.
*   **Mortality and the Dance of Death:** The powerful concluding motif of the "Totentanz" (Dance of Death) from St. Marien serves as a memento mori. It underscores the universal and leveling power of death, irrespective of social status or historical impact, and provides a poignant, self-aware acceptance of Wullenwever's ultimate fate.
