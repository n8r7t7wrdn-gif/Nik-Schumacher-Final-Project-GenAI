### Analysis of 'Die nagende Maus'

**1. PLOT SUMMARY:**
A Saxon journeyman arrives in Lübeck and asks his innkeeper about the city's emblematic landmark. The innkeeper recounts the legend of the "gnawing mouse": In ancient times, a rosebush at the choir of St. Marien Church symbolized Lübeck's freedom. When the city faced Danish threats, some citizens considered yielding for trade, but the Council relied on the rosebush as a symbol of resilience. However, the rosebush withered and died because a mouse had gnawed its roots, leading to the city's fall to the Danes. After Lübeck regained its freedom, the Council commemorated the event with a stone carving of the rosebush and mouse at St. Marien. Upon inspecting the carving, the journeyman notes two discrepancies: it depicts an *oak* stock, not a rosebush, and it appears *alive* and leafy, not withered. A debate ensues about historical accuracy versus narrative integrity, with the innkeeper ultimately stating that "what is made with art lives and greens forever, even if reality has withered and died," leaving the journeyman without a retort.

**2. SPATIAL REPRESENTATION OF LÜBECK:**
Lübeck is primarily represented through its iconic landmark, **St. Marien Church** (St. Mary's), specifically its **choir** facing **Mengstraße**. This precise topographical detail grounds the legend in a tangible, recognizable urban reality, inviting the reader to visualize a specific site. The **Herberge seines Handwerks** (the guild inn) serves as a traditional, communal space where local lore is transmitted, providing an intimate setting within the broader city.
Atmospherically, Lübeck is portrayed as a "stolze junge Stadt" (proud young city), evoking a sense of historical significance, resilience, and past struggles. The inn setting contributes to an authentic, old-world charm, a place where history is shared orally. Emotionally, the narrative conveys a blend of civic pride, historical vulnerability (the moment of losing courage and falling to the Danes), and eventual triumph and restoration of freedom. The city's fate is deeply intertwined with its symbolic objects, illustrating a profound connection between Lübeck's identity and its enduring narratives.

**3. THEMES & MOTIFS:**
*   **Tradition and Local Lore:** The entire story centers on the transmission and interpretation of a "Wahrzeichen der Stadt," making it a direct exploration of Lübeck's rich oral tradition and local legends. The innkeeper embodies the role of the cultural custodian.
*   **History and Memory:** The legend recounts a critical historical period – Lübeck's struggle for independence against the Danes. The debate between the journeyman and the innkeeper regarding the accuracy of the carving (rosebush vs. oak, withered vs. alive) highlights the fluid nature of historical memory and how it can be reshaped for narrative or artistic purposes.
*   **Art vs. Reality/Truth:** This is the central thematic tension. The journeyman's empirical observations challenge the factual basis of the artistic representation. The innkeeper's ultimate retort—"Was mit der Kunst gemacht ist, lebt und grünt für ewig, wenn auch die Wirklichkeit verdorrt und abgestorben ist"—elevates art above mere mimesis, asserting its power to eternalize meaning and truth beyond ephemeral, factual reality.
*   **National Identity and Independence:** The rosebush explicitly symbolizes the "Freiheit der Stadt" and its connection to the "Reich." The threat of Danish annexation and the internal debate among citizens prioritizing commerce over independence underscore the historical struggles for sovereignty and the constant negotiation of identity for a powerful Hanseatic city.
*   **Symbolism and Interpretation:** The rosebush/oak as a symbol of life and freedom, and the "nagende Maus" as a subtle, insidious internal threat, are potent motifs. The story also examines how these symbols are interpreted and, at times, adapted to create a more compelling or fitting legend ("besser in eine Sage passt").
*   **Skepticism vs. Belief:** The journeyman's persistent questioning represents a modern, critical, and empirical approach to history and art, contrasting with the innkeeper's more traditional, perhaps Romantic, appreciation of legend and art's ability to convey deeper, enduring truths.
