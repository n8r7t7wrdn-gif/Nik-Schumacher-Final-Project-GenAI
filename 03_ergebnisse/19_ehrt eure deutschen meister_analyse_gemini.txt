### 1. PLOT SUMMARY

On Palm Sunday 1884, following the death of the celebrated Lübeck poet Emanuel Geibel, his devoted friend, the broker Herr Schunk, passionately advocates for state honors. He confronts the city's Mayor, who initially displays pragmatic reluctance, citing protocol and financial concerns for proposals like half-mast flags on the Rathaus, tolling church bells from St. Marien, and a state funeral. Each of Schunk's escalating demands is met with resistance from the Mayor, who personifies cautious bureaucracy. However, a series of telegrams from powerful external figures—the Grand Duke of Mecklenburg-Schwerin, a Bavarian King, and the German Crown Prince Friedrich Wilhelm—expressing profound national grief and recognition for Geibel as a "great German man," gradually chips away at the Mayor's opposition. Overwhelmed by this external pressure, the Mayor ultimately concedes to all of Schunk’s requests, ensuring Geibel receives a grand state funeral, symbolizing a triumph of artistic recognition over municipal pragmatism.

### 2. SPATIAL REPRESENTATION OF LÜBECK

Lübeck is primarily represented as a proud, self-governing **"Freie und Hansestadt,"** steeped in its own traditions, protocols, and bureaucratic pragmatism.

*   **Topographically:** Key locations define the city's character. The **Rathaus** (City Hall) is the central administrative hub, its flag-waving becoming a symbol of official state recognition, initially resisted by the Mayor. The **Marienkirche** (St. Mary's Church) is invoked for its bells, which are meant to provide a solemn, spiritual, and auditory presence spreading "über Stadt und Land hin," thus signifying a public, communal grieving transcending mere administrative decree. The **Breite Straße** is mentioned as the tangible urban space where Herr Schunk observes the flying flags, grounding the abstract political victory in a concrete setting. The collective entity of "ganz Lübeck, die ganze Vaterstadt" following the procession emphasizes the city's collective identity in mourning.
*   **Atmospherically:** The narrative begins with the Mayor's somewhat detached, "unzeitig" feeling, reflecting a pragmatic atmosphere. This evolves to one of public solemnity and grandeur, evoked by the "ernsten Tönen" and "tiefen weihe- und wehevollen Tönen" of the bells and the envisioned splendor of the funeral procession. The "frischen Aprilwind" adds a touch of grounded realism to the historical event.
*   **Emotionally:** The city, through its Mayor, initially struggles between its inherent Hanseatic caution regarding state expenditures and its role within a larger German cultural and national identity. It is compelled to shift from private, individual grief (Schunk's) to a public, state-sanctioned act of remembrance, ultimately embracing its role as the "Vaterstadt" of a "großen Sohnes" and expressing collective pride and sorrow.

### 3. THEMES & MOTIFS

The story 'Ehrt eure deutschen Meister' explores several interconnected themes:

*   **Art vs. Bureaucracy/Commerce:** This central conflict pits the passionate defense of artistic legacy (represented by Herr Schunk's fervent advocacy for Geibel, a renowned poet) against the pragmatic, cost-conscious, and protocol-driven municipal bureaucracy, personified by the Mayor of Lübeck. The Mayor's initial reluctance to grant state honors, viewing Geibel as merely "a poet" and not a "Senator" or state official, highlights the tension between cultural value and administrative expediency, ultimately depicting art's triumph over narrow officialdom. The Mayor's physical "freezing" at the mention of "state costs" explicitly underscores the commercial mindset clashing with cultural commemoration.

*   **National Identity vs. Local Autonomy:** A significant tension arises from Lübeck's proud status as a "Freie und Hansestadt" with its distinct traditions and self-governing protocols, versus the burgeoning German national consciousness of the late 19th century. The Mayor's resistance to what he perceives as extraordinary municipal expenditures is only overcome by a cascade of telegrams from powerful national figures (a Grand Duke, a King, and the German Crown Prince), who explicitly frame Geibel's death as a national loss ("ganz Deutschland in Trauer versetzt") and Geibel as a "great German man." This illustrates the absorption of regional identity into a broader German national narrative, where national heroes transcend local protocols.

*   **Tradition and Commemoration:** The story's title itself, echoing Wagner's *Meistersinger von Nürnberg*, immediately invokes the theme of honoring "German Masters" and upholding cultural heritage. Schunk's vivid vision of a grand funeral procession blends historical civic pomp—with "rote Ratsdiener," "Staatskutschen," and "eingewebten roten Adlern"—with a modern imperative for national commemoration. This reflects a desire to maintain continuity with the past through ritual while celebrating a figure of contemporary national significance, linking civic pride to a shared cultural legacy.

*   **The Power of External Authority:** The narrative demonstrates how political pressure from higher national authorities effectively dictates municipal actions. The Mayor's concessions are not born from conviction or emotional persuasion by Schunk, but solely from the hierarchical weight of telegrams from powerful figures of the newly unified German Empire, highlighting the limits of local autonomy when confronted with broader state interests.
