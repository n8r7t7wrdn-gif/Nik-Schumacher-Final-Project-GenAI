### Analysis of Otto Anthes: "Die Zehnte"

**1. PLOT SUMMARY:**
Gerd Hartog, a melancholic Lübeck woodcarver, endures a cheerless life with his constantly complaining and gluttonous wife, Philippine. His artistic talent is praised but unused, as he lacks salesmanship. An opportunity arises when he is commissioned to direct and design costumes for a play based on the Ten Virgins from the Gospel, celebrating a peace treaty between Lübeck and Holstein. During rehearsals, Gerd, a repressed artist, becomes captivated by Gred Eicke, a spirited patrician's daughter playing the "most foolish" virgin. At the celebratory feast, Gred provocatively dances on a table, declaring "I am the flame! Don't touch me!" and then seeks Gerd's help to escape a pursuing count. Hidden in a dark room, she boldly kisses Gerd, proclaiming, "I kiss a duke now," before abandoning him. Inspired, Gerd is commissioned to carve the ten virgins for a church. He secretly creates the tenth, a "foolish" virgin, as a vivid portrait of Gred. Philippine discovers it, furiously accuses him of an affair, and smashes the statue. Overwhelmed by disgust for his wife and the realization of his lost life, Gerd commits suicide by drowning in the Trave, taking the unbroken statue with him. The legend of the missing tenth virgin and Gred Eicke's spirit living on in Lübeck's charmingly foolish girls endures.

**2. SPATIAL REPRESENTATION OF LÜBECK:**

*   **Topographical:** Lübeck is presented as a city of historical depth and distinct social strata, marked by specific, evocative locations:
    *   **Glockengießerstraße:** Gerd Hartog's residence and workshop, a typical artisan street, representing his constrained domestic and creative life.
    *   **Tanzhaus / Rathaus / Altes Haus:** The bustling centers of civic and social life, where the play is performed and the feast takes place. These spaces symbolize power, public spectacle, and the intersection of politics and entertainment. The "Altes Haus" with its hidden door provides a clandestine space for Gerd and Gred's intimate moment.
    *   **Sandstraße:** The residence of Ratsherr Eicke and Gred, indicating a patrician district, distinct from the craftsman's quarter.
    *   **Burgkloster / Burgkirche:** The sacred space where Gerd's artwork is intended to be displayed, signifying a connection between art, religion, and public beautification.
    *   **Hundestraße / Breite Straße / Trave:** These streets and the river form the tragic path of Gerd's final journey, leading him from the social constraints of the city to the liberating yet fatal embrace of the water. The Trave, a prominent feature of Lübeck, becomes the silent witness to his despair and ultimate escape.
    *   **St.-Annen-Straße / Museum:** The present-day location of the remaining statues, connecting the historical narrative to contemporary Lübeck as a city preserving its past and artistic heritage, while also fostering the legend.

*   **Atmospheric:** The city's atmosphere is initially depicted as one of rigid social order and conventionality, where artistic talent struggles against commercial demands and personal unhappiness. However, through the feast and Gerd's artistic awakening, a hidden undercurrent of passion, longing, and social transgression emerges. Lübeck transforms from a static backdrop into a dynamic stage for human drama, where ancient traditions (peace treaties, religious plays) mingle with individual desires and societal pressures.

*   **Emotional:** For Gerd, Lübeck is a place of quiet suffering and artistic refuge (his workshop). His initial interaction with the city is one of resignation. However, Gred Eicke's presence imbues the city's public spaces with a charged energy, reflecting Gerd's reawakened youth and desire. The city's grand public events become the backdrop for his intense, yet ultimately tragic, personal transformation. The legend at the end suggests Lübeck is also a city where beauty, folly, and artistic spirit can persist, even if the artist himself is lost.

**3. THEMES & MOTIFS:**

*   **Art vs. Commerce/Philistinism:** This is a central conflict. Gerd Hartog is a master craftsman ("schweigsamer Könner") who creates art for its own sake, accumulating "unverkaufte Bildwerke." The world, however, values "glatter Schwätzer" who can "anpreisen" their wares. His wife, Philippine, embodies philistinism, dismissing his art as "sitzengebliebene Weibs-bilder" and focusing solely on the monetary gain from the commission. His eventual suicide highlights the artist's struggle to maintain integrity and passion in a commercially driven and unsympathetic world.

*   **Unfulfilled Life / Repressed Desire:** Gerd's life is marked by deep unhappiness stemming from his "übel verheiratet" state. His "stille Munterkeit" has "ins Geheimste verkrochen." Gred Eicke serves as an external catalyst, awakening his "frühbegrabene Jugend" and reigniting his passion and capacity for joy and desire. Her kiss, a moment of profound, albeit brief, connection, represents the emotional and sensual life he was denied. His death is a tragic culmination of this repressed life bursting forth and then being violently extinguished.

*   **Bourgeoisie / Social Hierarchy:** The story clearly delineates the social strata of Lübeck. Gerd is a respected but subordinate craftsman. The "Grafen von Holstein" represent the external nobility, while the "Ratsherren" (e.g., Eicke) embody Lübeck's powerful patrician class. Gred Eicke, as a Ratsherrentochter, embodies both beauty and social transgression, navigating the expectations of her class with a rebellious spirit. The phrase "Ich küsse jetzt einen Herzog" is significant, not only referencing Gerd Hartog's surname (Herzog = duke) but also asserting Gred's defiance of the count and her momentary elevation of the artist.

*   **The Artist's Muse / Inspiration:** Gred Eicke functions as Gerd's ultimate muse. She inspires him to create his most beautiful and authentic work – the tenth virgin, a direct, passionate portrayal of her. Her essence, "die straffe Anmut, dasselbe schlanke Strecken der Glieder," captivates him, transforming his artistic process from dutiful carving to an expression of his deepest feelings.

*   **Tragedy of the Artist:** Gerd's story is a profound tragedy of an artist whose personal and creative life are in agonizing conflict with his circumstances. His artistic awakening and brief experience of joy and passion ultimately lead to his destruction, exacerbated by his wife's lack of understanding and destructive jealousy. His suicide is a desperate act to protect his art and his innermost self from a life that denied him true fulfillment.

*   **The Power of Art / Immortality through Legend:** Despite the physical destruction and disappearance of the tenth statue, the story concludes with the enduring legend. Gred Eicke's spirit, characterized by "hübsch und so entzückend töricht," lives on in the collective memory of Lübeck and in the faces of its young women. This suggests that true art, even when lost, can achieve a form of immortality, transcending the artist's tragic fate and material form.
